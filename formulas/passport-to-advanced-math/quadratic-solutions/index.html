Product of Solutions

\[ \text{Product} = \frac{c}{a} \]

INSTANTLY find what the solutions multiply to. Essential when the SAT asks for the product of roots.

Example Problem

"What is the product of the solutions to \(3x^2 - 7x + 12 = 0\)?"

üö´ School Way (90s)

1. Use Quadratic Formula: \(x = \frac{7 \pm \sqrt{49 - 144}}{6}\)
2. Get complex roots: \(x = \frac{7 \pm i\sqrt{95}}{6}\)
3. Multiply roots using \((a+bi)(a-bi)\)...
4. Eventually find **4**.

‚úÖ Practix Way (2s)

Identify \(a=3, c=12\).
Product = \(c/a = 12/3\)
**Result: 4**
No roots needed.

Sum of Solutions

\[ \text{Sum} = -\frac{b}{a} \]

Instantly find what the solutions add up to. Don't waste time solving for individual roots.

Example Problem

"What is the sum of the solutions to \(2x^2 + 8x - 5 = 0\)?"

üö´ School Way (60s)

1. Quadratic Formula: \(x = \frac{-8 \pm \sqrt{64 - (-40)}}{4}\)
2. Roots: \(\frac{-8 \pm \sqrt{104}}{4}\)
3. Roots: \(-2 \pm \frac{\sqrt{26}}{2}\)
4. Add: \((-2 + \frac{\sqrt{26}}{2}) + (-2 - \frac{\sqrt{26}}{2})\)
5. Find **-4**.

‚úÖ Practix Way (2s)

Identify \(a=2, b=8\).
Sum = \(-b/a = -8/2\)
**Result: -4**
Immediate points.

The Discriminant

\[ D = b^2 - 4ac \]

\(D > 0\): 2 Real Solutions
\(D = 0\): 1 Real Solution
\(D < 0\): No Real Solutions

Example Problem

"How many real solutions does \(x^2 + 4x + 4 = 0\) have?"

üö´ School Way (30s)

1. Try to factor: \((x+2)(x+2) = 0\)
2. Find root: \(x = -2\)
3. Identity one unique solution.
4. Find **1**.

‚úÖ Practix Way (5s)

Check \(b^2 - 4ac\):
\(4^2 - 4(1)(4) = 16 - 16 = 0\)
**Result: 1 Real Solution**
Faster for hard numbers.

üéì The Proof: Vieta's Formulas

Why does \(-b/a\) work? It comes from the structure of a factored quadratic.

1. Any quadratic with roots \(r_1\) and \(r_2\) can be written as:

\( a(x - r_1)(x - r_2) = 0 \)

2. Expanding this gives:

\( a(x^2 - (r_1 + r_2)x + r_1r_2) = 0 \)
\( ax^2 - a(r_1 + r_2)x + ar_1r_2 = 0 \)

3. Comparing this to the standard form \(ax^2 + bx + c = 0\):

  • \(b = -a(r_1 + r_2) \Rightarrow \mathbf{r_1 + r_2 = -b/a}\)
  • \(c = ar_1r_2 \Rightarrow \mathbf{r_1r_2 = c/a}\)

This is why you don't need to solve for the roots‚Äîthe sum and product are "baked into" the coefficients themselves.
