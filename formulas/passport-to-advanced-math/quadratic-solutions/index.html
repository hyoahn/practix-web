Product of Solutions

\[ \text{Product} = \frac{c}{a} \]

INSTANTLY find what the solutions multiply to. Essential when the SAT asks for the product of roots.

Example: "What is the product of the solutions to \(3x^2 - 7x + 12 = 0\)?"

Show Solution & Analysis
üö´ School Way (Rigorous)

1. Setup Quadratic Formula:
\(x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}\)
Identify \(a=3, b=-7, c=12\).
\(x = \frac{-(-7) \pm \sqrt{(-7)^2 - 4(3)(12)}}{2(3)}\)
2. Simplify:
\(x = \frac{7 \pm \sqrt{49 - 144}}{6} = \frac{7 \pm \sqrt{-95}}{6}\)
3. Define Complex Roots:
\(x_1 = \frac{7 + i\sqrt{95}}{6}, x_2 = \frac{7 - i\sqrt{95}}{6}\)
4. Calculate Product:
\(x_1 \cdot x_2 = \left(\frac{7 + i\sqrt{95}}{6}\right)\left(\frac{7 - i\sqrt{95}}{6}\right)\)
Numerator: \((7+i\sqrt{95})(7-i\sqrt{95}) = 49 - (i\sqrt{95})^2 = 49 - (-95) = 144\).
Denominator: \(6 \cdot 6 = 36\).
5. Result: \(144 / 36 = 4\).

‚úÖ Practix Way (Optimal)

**Step 1:** Identify coefficients.
\(a=3, c=12\).
**Step 2:** Apply shortcut.
Product = \(c/a = 12/3\).
**Result: 4**
Time saved: 85+ seconds.

Sum of Solutions

\[ \text{Sum} = -\frac{b}{a} \]

Instantly find what the solutions add up to. Don't waste time solving for individual roots.

Example: "What is the sum of the solutions to \(2x^2 + 8x - 5 = 0\)?"

Show Solution & Analysis
üö´ School Way (Rigorous)

1. Setup Quadratic Formula:
Identify \(a=2, b=8, c=-5\).
\(x = \frac{-8 \pm \sqrt{8^2 - 4(2)(-5)}}{2(2)}\)
2. Simplify Discriminant:
\(x = \frac{-8 \pm \sqrt{64 + 40}}{4} = \frac{-8 \pm \sqrt{104}}{4}\)
3. Simplify Terms:
\(x = \frac{-8}{4} \pm \frac{\sqrt{104}}{4} = -2 \pm \frac{2\sqrt{26}}{4} = -2 \pm \frac{\sqrt{26}}{2}\)
4. Define Roots:
\(x_1 = -2 + \frac{\sqrt{26}}{2}, x_2 = -2 - \frac{\sqrt{26}}{2}\)
5. Calculate Sum:
\(x_1 + x_2 = (-2 + \frac{\sqrt{26}}{2}) + (-2 - \frac{\sqrt{26}}{2})\)
Radicals cancel out: \(-2 - 2 = -4\).
Result: -4

‚úÖ Practix Way (Optimal)

**Step 1:** Identify coefficients.
\(a=2, b=8\).
**Step 2:** Apply shortcut.
Sum = \(-b/a = -8/2\).
**Result: -4**
Done in seconds.

The Discriminant

\[ D = b^2 - 4ac \]

\(D > 0\): 2 Real Solutions
\(D = 0\): 1 Real Solution
\(D < 0\): No Real Solutions

Example: "How many real solutions does \(x^2 + 4x + 4 = 0\) have?"

Show Solution & Analysis
üö´ School Way (Rigorous)

1. Attempt Factoring:
We look for two numbers that multiply to 4 and add to 4.
\(x^2 + 4x + 4 = (x+2)(x+2) = (x+2)^2\).
2. Solve for Roots:
\(x + 2 = 0 \implies x = -2\).
3. Count Solutions:
There is only one distinct value for \(x\).
Result: 1 Real Solution

‚úÖ Practix Way (Optimal)

**Step 1:** Calculate the Discriminant \(D\).
\(b^2 - 4ac = 4^2 - 4(1)(4) = 16 - 16 = 0\).
**Step 2:** Interpret result.
\(D = 0 \implies \text{Exactly 1 real solution}\).
**Result: 1**
Essential for equations that don't factor easily.

üéì The Rigorous Proof: Vieta's Derivation

Let's derive these shortcuts starting from the most fundamental definition of a quadratic equation.

Step 1: The Standard Form
Every quadratic equation of degree 2 can be expressed as:

\( ax^2 + bx + c = 0 \)

Assuming \( a \neq 0 \), we divide the entire equation by \( a \):

\( x^2 + \frac{b}{a}x + \frac{c}{a} = 0 \)

Step 2: The Linear Factors
If \( x_1 \) and \( x_2 \) are the solutions (roots) of this equation, then by the Factor Theorem, the polynomial must be divisible by \( (x - x_1) \) and \( (x - x_2) \). Thus:

\( (x - x_1)(x - x_2) = 0 \)

Step 3: Expansion by FOIL
We multiply the linear factors to return to quadratic form:

\( x \cdot x - x \cdot x_2 - x \cdot x_1 + x_1 \cdot x_2 = 0 \)
\( x^2 - (x_1 + x_2)x + x_1x_2 = 0 \)

Step 4: Coefficient Matching
We now have two expressions for the same normalized quadratic. They must be identical term-for-term:

\( x^2 + \left(\mathbf{\frac{b}{a}}\right)x + \left(\mathbf{\frac{c}{a}}\right) = x^2 - \left(\mathbf{x_1 + x_2}\right)x + \left(\mathbf{x_1x_2}\right) \)

Step 5: Theoretical Conclusion

  • The Sum Rule: Matching the \( x \)-coefficients:
    \( \frac{b}{a} = -(x_1 + x_2) \implies \mathbf{x_1 + x_2 = -\frac{b}{a}} \)
  • The Product Rule: Matching the constant terms:
    \( \frac{c}{a} = x_1x_2 \implies \mathbf{x_1x_2 = \frac{c}{a}} \)

This proof demonstrates that the sum and product of the roots are inherently linked to the coefficients of the polynomial. This is a foundational principle of algebra known as Vieta's Formulas.
